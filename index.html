About Bukovina

Bukovina is located in the Nothern Part of Romania. TTe region is divided in Ukrainian Bukovyna, Romanian Bucovina, German Bucowina. The territory consists a segment of the Northeastern Carpathian Mountains (Oriental Carpathians) divided after 1947, between Romania and Ukraine. Settled by both Ukrainians (Ruthenians) and Romanians (Moldavians), the region became an integral part of the principality of Moldavia in the 14th century. Suceava, in the south of the territory, was the capital of Moldavia from the late 14th to the mid-16th century. Bukovina acquired its own name and identity only in 1774, when it was ceded to Austria by the Turks, who then controlled the whole region of Moldavia (which included the Republic of Moldova).

Austrians viewed Bukovina as a strategic link between Transylvania and Galicia. Between 1786–1849 the region was administered as a part of Galicia and then as a duchy and a separate crown land. Austria also developed Bukovina’s chief city, Czernowitz (now Chernivtsi, Ukraine), into an important educational and commercial centre. The Austrians kept a balance between the various ethnic groups in Bukovina; the population was almost solidly Ukrainian in the north and Romanian in the south, while in the towns the population included Germans, Polish, and Jews.

In 1878 Romania achieved its independece, it sought unification with Bukovina. It did so because Bukovina was not only the historical cradle of the Moldavian principality but also the repository of the finest examples of Romanian art and architecture, having unique painted monastic churches of the 15th and 16th centuries. Romania occupied Bukovina when Austria-Hungary collapsed in 1918.

Ukranians tried to incorporate their districts in northern Bukovina into the Western Ukrainian National Republic, however Romania gained control of the whole province (Treaty of Saint-Germain; 1919) and pursued a Rumanization policy there. In June 1940 the Soviet Union occupied the northern part of Bukovina, but Romania temporarily regained this territory as an ally of Germany. Bukovina was later lost through the invasion of the U.S.S.R. in 1941, when the soviet troops retook the northern districts in 1944.

Northern Bukovina (as Chernivtsi oblast [province]) became part of the Ukrainian Soviet Socialist Republic under the peace treaty of 1947; the ancient Moldavian capital Suceava and the surrounding area, including the most famous of the monasteries, became part of the Romanian People’s Republic.
